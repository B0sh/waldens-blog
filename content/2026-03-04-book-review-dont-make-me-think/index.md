![Don't make me think book](https://static.waldenperry.com/2026/dont-make-me-think.jpeg)

This [book](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don%27t_Make_Me_Think) is famous in the world of web design and usability, and after reading it for myself I can see why. I read the 2nd edition from 2005. You'd think that in 21 years everything with the web would be completely different, but I found the vast majority of content timeless here. The takeaways in here are much more about how to think about usability than tricks of the trade or the latest hot design. (You won't find any code snippets in this book!)

It's a quick read at only a few hours, but so much stood out to me. For example, the comparison between websites and the real world was particularly interesting. Let's say you were shopping in a store, as you walk around searching for `$SOMETHING` you can feel spacially that you are moving through the world, searching from east to west or whatever. But on a website, every click is functionally an instant teleportation device. The point here is that good design needs to add elements to show *where* you are on the site.

Krug talks about a hierarchy in web design. A website logo and page titles are so important because they ground yourself throughout the website journey. It also affirms subconsciously that "Hey, you're still in the right place."

The more jarring the difference between pages is, the user is forced to readjust and think deeply about what happened. I can think of many examples where I'm on a site with a certain look, and then after accessing some feature, suddenly the entire layout changes. I don't think I've ever consciously realized this as something to watch out for as a web developer. 

Designers and programmers tend to think that people using their awesome website are completely rational, perfectly evaluate every option present and pick the best one. That's a fantasy. People actually look until they find something that seems like it might work, try that, and if it doesn't go back and try again. When I stopped and thought about it more, this matches my browsing behavior as well. With modern SPAs extra attention has to be paid to the back button experience so as not to punish people for starting over.

### User Testing

A huge topic is user testing. Krug advocates for every serious website to do some amount of user testing. Every time I've done user testing in my career it's been very insightful and I usually wish I could do more. 

> Testing reminds you that not everyone thinks the way you do, knows what you know, uses the Web the way you do.

He mentions how testing doesn't have to be a big deal thing that takes a ton of time. In fact it's better to have more, smaller sessions. If the first session uncovers some major problem, then issues further below might not even be reached. Addressing issues as you go allows for more polish down the stack of your website. 

It's too new for this book, but in the modern era we do have this concept of automated A/B testing. I think that the automated aspect of these lead to a hyper optimization mentality where nobody is actually in charge of the entire experience of using the website. Not that I have any experience working with these kinds of tests, just being experimented *on*.

### Don't make me think!

As soon as I finished I could tell why this work is a timeless classic on usability. "Don't make me think" really is a great motto for web usability. As a programmer, it's easy to have a tendency to get bored with the site you've been spending months developing, only to start adding quirky features and breaking conventions for fun. It's not that you shouldn't do those things, but the more you break convention, the more burden you put upon your user to understand what the hell is going on over in [your world](https://waldens.world/). I honestly can't wait to review some of my designs with the principles from this book in mind.
